Locality · Montgomery County

Working in Montgomery County: the local layer on top of the state pathway.

The statewide steps still apply in full. This page covers what changes once a job sits inside Montgomery County, where registration and permits are handled township by township rather than by the county.

Montgomery County does not license contractors or issue building permits.

The county government's permitting role covers public-health and environmental matters such as food service, camps, pools, wells, and sewage. It does not license contractors and does not issue residential building permits. Those functions sit with each municipality.

Montgomery County has 62 municipalities, and each sets its own contractor-registration and permit rules, fees, and submission portals. There is no single county form or county license. The contractor must identify the exact township, borough, or the Municipality of Norristown where the job site sits and follow that municipality's process.

How permits work here

The Uniform Construction Code is enforced locally, often through a third-party agency.

Over 90% of Pennsylvania municipalities enforce the UCC themselves or through a state-certified third-party code agency that the municipality hires. In Montgomery County, many townships route plan review, permits, and inspections through such an agency, so the office that issues a permit may be a private firm acting for the township. Confirm who the municipality uses before applying.

Example. Douglass Township names its outside code agency directly on its building and zoning page (Kraft Municipal Group). Other townships use firms such as Barry Isett & Associates or Building Inspection Underwriters of Pennsylvania. The authoritative, current list of certified agencies is maintained by the PA Department of Labor & Industry.
Municipalities that require local registration

Six Montgomery County municipalities confirmed to require a local contractor registration or license.

This is a representative sample, not the full county. Several fee figures below come from the municipalities' published pages but should be confirmed on the live page at the time of application, because local fees change annually. Each card links to the official source.

Registration required

Lower Merion Township

Requires a township contractor's license for commercial contractors and residential home builders. A contractor registered as a Pennsylvania HIC is treated as not needing the township license for home-improvement work. An insurance certificate is required.

Abington Township

"All contractors who perform work within Abington Township are required to register with the Township," through an online portal. Workers' compensation coverage is referenced in the process.

License required

Cheltenham Township

Requires a general contractor license, reported at $85 plus permit fees, with separate fees for specialty trades. Working before permits are issued triggers a triple permit fee. Confirm current figures on the township page.

Annual registration

Upper Merion Township

All on-site contractors on commercial work must register annually; residential home-improvement contractors supply their PA HIC number; plumbers and electricians register regardless. A workers' comp affidavit is provided for the self-employed. Fee reported around $30 plus a sticker fee; confirm on the page.

Registration required

Municipality of Norristown

"Prior to doing any work in the Municipality of Norristown or obtaining a permit, contractors are required to register with the Municipality." Residential contractors are limited to the work their PA Attorney General HIC authorization permits. License fees are tiered by quarter; confirm current figures.

How to find the rule for the municipality you are working in.

1. Confirm the municipality of the job site.The mailing-address town is not always the municipality of jurisdiction. Confirm the township, borough, or Norristown that actually governs the parcel.
2. Find that municipality's code-enforcement or building department page.Look for "contractor registration," "contractor licensing," or "permits."
3. Determine the three things that vary.Whether it requires separate local registration or a license, whether it accepts the PA HIC in place of a local license, and whether permits and inspections go through a third-party agency.
4. Confirm the current fee on the official fee schedule.Local fees change annually; use the municipality's own current schedule, not a third-party summary.
5. Confirm statewide PA HIC status separately.The Attorney General registration applies to home-improvement work of $5,000 or more per year regardless of municipality. See the AG walkthrough.
Verify before relying.
