Pennsylvania · Residential construction

The clear pathway to operating as a contractor in Pennsylvania.

Every step below is drawn from the controlling statute or the issuing government agency, with a link to the source. It is built to take a skilled tradesman from "I want to run my own construction company" to lawfully open for business.

See the steps Go to the official portals

First, the fact that changes everything: Pennsylvania has no statewide general contractor license.

The Commonwealth does not license or certify general construction contractors at the state level. In the Department of Labor & Industry's own words: "The Commonwealth of Pennsylvania currently has no licensure or certification requirements for most construction contractors (or their employees)."

What this means in practice. For residential work, the law requires registration as a Home Improvement Contractor with the Attorney General, which is an administrative registration, not a competency license. Where an actual contractor license exists, it is issued by the municipality, not the state. Cities such as Pittsburgh and Philadelphia run their own contractor-license programs, and those apply to new construction and commercial work. So the answer to "does he need to be licensed?" is: register with the state, and then check the license rules of every city, borough, or township where he takes work. The pathway below covers both.

The pathway

Eleven steps, in the order a contractor should take them.

Each card states what to do, why it matters, whether it is required by law or merely advisable, the cost, and a link you can act on or verify against the source.

1

Form the business entity

Advisable

Form a Pennsylvania LLC by filing a Certificate of Organization (DSCB:15-8821) with the Department of State, with a docketing statement, and maintain a registered office in the Commonwealth.

An LLC separates business liability from personal assets. A sole proprietor has no shield, so a construction-defect or jobsite-injury claim can reach the owner's home and savings. For a trade with real liability exposure, the LLC is the reason to file rather than operate bare.

Cost$125 filing fee

2

Register the business name (if using a trade name)

Required if applicable

If he operates under any name other than his own legal name or the LLC's exact registered name, file a Registration of Fictitious Name (DSCB:54-311) with the Department of State.

Using a trade name such as "Smith & Sons Construction" without registering it is a violation and undermines the right to enforce contracts under that name. Operating strictly under the legal or exact entity name needs no filing.

3

Get a federal EIN from the IRS

Advisable

Apply directly to the IRS for an Employer Identification Number after the entity is formed. It is required to hire employees or run a multi-member LLC, and banks and counterparties expect it in nearly every case.

The EIN is free and issued immediately online. The IRS warns: "Beware of websites that charge for an EIN. You never have to pay a fee for an EIN." Use only the IRS site.

CostFree
StatusRequired if hiring; advisable otherwise
Apply for an EIN (IRS)
4

Register for Pennsylvania taxes (myPATH)

Required as applicable

Register state tax accounts through myPATH. A construction contractor is treated as the consumer of the materials he installs: he pays sales tax on his own material purchases and does not charge customers sales tax on construction labor or on materials permanently affixed to the property.

A separate sales-tax license is needed only if he also makes taxable retail sales or provides taxable services. If he hires employees, he must register an employer withholding account here as well.

CostNo registration fee
StatusRequired for any tax that applies
Register at myPATH Contractor tax guidance
5

Register as a Home Improvement Contractor (Attorney General)

Required

Register with the Office of Attorney General before performing any home improvement work. This is mandatory for anyone whose home improvements total $5,000 or more in the previous taxable year. The statute is blunt: "No person shall hold himself out as a contractor, nor shall a person perform any home improvement without first registering with the bureau."

This is the central residential requirement in Pennsylvania. The registration number must appear on all advertising, contracts, estimates, and proposals. Registration is valid two years and renews biennially. (The statute sets the fee at $50; the Attorney General currently charges $100.)

Cost$100, non-refundable, every 2 years
StatusRequired at or above $5,000/year
Register at the AG portal Application walkthrough & documents AG registration FAQ HICPA §§ 2, 3, 5, 6
6

Carry the required liability insurance

Required

HICPA requires a home improvement contractor to maintain liability insurance of at least $50,000 for personal injury and at least $50,000 for property damage. Proof accompanies the registration application (§ 4(a)(1)(ix)), and the contract must contain the covenant to maintain it (§ 7(a)(11)).

$50,000/$50,000 is a legal floor, not a market-adequate figure. Owners and general contractors routinely require $1,000,000 per occurrence and $2,000,000 aggregate by contract, and some city licenses set their own minimum (Philadelphia requires $500,000 per occurrence). Carry the floor to register; carry the market limits to win work and to be genuinely protected.

CostVaries by carrier and limits
Status$50k/$50k required; higher limits advisable
HICPA §§ 4, 7 Market limit norms
7

Get EPA lead certification (RRP) for pre-1978 homes

Required

A firm doing renovation, repair, or painting that disturbs paint in housing built before 1978 must be an EPA Lead-Safe Certified Firm and assign a certified renovator. Pennsylvania is administered directly by EPA, so the firm certifies with EPA.

"Federal law requires all renovation, repair, and painting firms (including sole proprietorships) working in housing ... built before 1978, to be certified." Given how much of Pennsylvania's housing stock predates 1978, a residential contractor should treat this as a near-certainty. Certification lasts five years.

Cost$300, valid 5 years
StatusRequired for covered pre-1978 work

8

Carry workers' compensation (once he hires)

Required if employees

Pennsylvania makes workers' compensation mandatory for any employer with at least one employee. A solo owner is not required to cover himself, but hiring even one worker triggers coverage for that worker. The mandate is in the Workers' Compensation Act (77 P.S. § 501).

Going without it is a third-degree misdemeanor, and a third-degree felony if intentional, with every day a separate offense, plus civil exposure to the Uninsured Employers Guaranty Fund. This is not a corner to cut.

CostPremium varies by payroll and class
StatusRequired with one or more employees
DLI employer information Workers' comp program
9

Meet OSHA jobsite safety obligations

Required

A construction employer must comply with the OSHA construction standards (29 C.F.R. Part 1926) and their hazard-specific training. The OSHA 10-hour and 30-hour Outreach cards are not themselves federally required, but states, municipalities, owners, and general contractors frequently require the card to work a job.

OSHA says plainly that "OSHA does not require the training," yet the card is a common contractual prerequisite. Treat compliance with the standards as mandatory and the 10/30 card as a practical door-opener.

10

Comply with HICPA's contract rules on every job

Required

A home improvement contract is unenforceable against the owner unless it is in writing, legible, and contains the registration number, a description of the work and materials, and the other terms HICPA lists. The owner has three business days to rescind without penalty. For contracts over $5,000, no deposit may exceed one-third of the price (or one-third plus the cost of special-order materials).

These are conditions of getting paid and staying out of consumer-protection trouble. Changes need a written change order signed by both parties, and contracts must carry the Bureau of Consumer Protection line (1-888-520-6680) and the statement "Registration does not imply endorsement."

CostNone (drafting discipline)
StatusRequired for enforceability
HICPA §§ 7, 9
11

Check municipal licensing and pull local permits

Required, varies by municipality

Building permits and inspections run through the Pennsylvania Uniform Construction Code (UCC), administered locally; over 90% of the state's 2,562 municipalities enforce it themselves or through a certified third-party agency. This is also where an actual general contractor license lives. Pittsburgh issues a General Contractor License for commercial-permit work and new one- and two-family dwellings; Philadelphia issues a Contractor License, with a narrower home-improvement pathway for work on existing one- or two-family homes.

For a new construction company, this is the step that answers "do I need a license?" The answer is set city by city. Electrical, plumbing, and HVAC work is also licensed locally, often requiring a separately licensed tradesperson. Verify the rules of every municipality where he works before bidding.

On bonding. Pennsylvania imposes no statewide surety-bond requirement on residential contractors. Bonds appear only through a particular municipal license, a permit or right-of-way bond, or a project's performance and payment bonds. A surety bond is not insurance: if the surety pays a claim, the contractor must repay it. Advertising "bonded" is fine only if a bond is actually in place.
